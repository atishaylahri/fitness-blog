Wearing the wrong shoe is the most common mistake in the gym, and it shows up in your numbers. Cushioned running shoes compress under load and turn every squat into a balance drill, while purely barefoot-style shoes punish your knees during box jumps and sprints. The right shoe — flat, firm, and matched to your training style — adds pounds to your lifts within a week of wearing them.

We tested across three categories: dedicated lifting shoes with elevated heels, flat cross-trainers for general gym work, and hybrid trainers that handle both lifting and conditioning. The picks below cover lifters from the rack pull to the rower.

## Nike Romaleos 4

The Nike Romaleos 4 are the dedicated weightlifting shoe most serious lifters end up in. A 20mm rigid heel locks you into a deep squat position, the dual-strap closure eliminates lateral foot movement, and the wide, hard sole transfers force directly into the platform. Not the shoe to do anything other than squat, clean, and snatch in.

Who it suits: Olympic weightlifters, anyone whose ankle mobility limits squat depth, and serious raw squatters. Typical price is $200–$220.

## Nike Metcon 9

The Metcon line has been the default CrossFit and general-training shoe for nearly a decade, and the Metcon 9 refines what already worked. A firm flat midfoot for stability under load, a slightly cushioned forefoot for short runs and jumps, and a grippy outsole for rope climbs. The trade-off: still not ideal for runs over a mile.

Who it suits: CrossFit athletes, HIIT class regulars, and anyone whose training spans lifting and conditioning in a single session. Typical price is $140–$160.

## Reebok Nano X4

Reebok invented the cross-trainer category and the Nano X4 is the latest evolution. Slightly more forgiving than the Metcon, with a more comfortable forefoot for casual wear, while still firm enough to support heavy back squats. The toe box is wider than the Metcon — a real plus for lifters with wide feet.

Who it suits: CrossFit and HIIT athletes with wide feet, anyone whose gym shoe doubles as casual wear, and Metcon owners looking for more comfort. Typical price is $130–$150.

## Adidas Powerlift 5

The Powerlift 5 is the entry-level lifting shoe most lifters should actually buy first. A 15mm heel — slightly lower than the Romaleos — gives you most of the squat benefit at a third of the price. The synthetic upper is not as durable as a $200 shoe, but the platform is genuinely flat and firm.

Who it suits: lifters trying their first heeled shoe, intermediate squatters, and anyone curious about lifting shoes without committing to a top-tier price. Typical price is $90–$120.

## Vivobarefoot Primus Lite III

If your training is heavy on deadlifts, kettlebell work, and ground-based movement, a barefoot-style shoe like the Primus Lite III delivers what zero-drop should: full ground feel, a wide toe splay, and no compressed midsole between you and the platform. Bad for running, perfect for pulling.

Who it suits: deadlift specialists, powerlifters who want a single shoe for pulls and accessories, and anyone whose training is lift-and-walk rather than lift-and-run. Typical price is $140–$170.

## Final Verdict

For most men whose training includes a mix of lifting and conditioning, the **Nike Metcon 9** or **Reebok Nano X4** are the right buys of 2026 — both are versatile enough to be your only gym shoe. Dedicated squatters and weightlifters should add a heeled shoe; start with the **Adidas Powerlift 5** before upgrading to the **Romaleos 4**. Deadlift specialists should ditch shoes with any cushion at all and go barefoot-style. The single biggest upgrade most lifters can make is simply not training in running shoes — anything on this list will improve your numbers within a session.
